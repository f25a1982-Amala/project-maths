The Ripening Challenge

Musang King (D197) Starch-to-Sugar Ripening Kinetics

Musang King durians are harvested strictly upon natural drop. Once dropped, metabolic respiration surges. Alpha-amylase enzymes rapidly hydrolyze insoluble starches into soluble reducing sugars (sucrose, fructose, and glucose), measured in degrees Brix (°Brix).

By calculating the first derivative $f'(x)$, agro-product technologists pinpoint the precise inflection point of peak enzymatic conversion. This marks the brief golden hour for cryogenic nitrogen freezing (-196°C) to prevent over-fermentation and preserve organic volatiles for export.

The Mathematical Model

Soluble Sugar Content: $f(x)$
f(x) = -0.1x³ + 0.9x² + 2x + 10

Outputs pulp sugar concentration in °Brix, where x represents days post-harvest ($0 \le x \le 7$).

Instantaneous Conversion Rate: $f'(x)$
f'(x) = -0.3x² + 1.8x + 2

Derived via the Power Rule. Represents conversion rate in °Brix per day.

Step-By-Step Derivation:
f'(x) = d/dx [-0.1x³ + 0.9x² + 2x + 10]
= (-0.1 × 3x²) + (0.9 × 2x) + (2 × 1) + 0
= -0.3x² + 1.8x + 2

Study Guide: Post-Harvest Processing Mathematics

1. Locating Peak Amylase Velocity The second derivative $f''(x) = -0.6x + 1.8$ equals zero when $x = 3.0$ days. This mathematically proves the inflection point of $f(x)$—the absolute peak rate of sugar generation.
2. Nitrogen Cryo-Freezing When the derivative starts dropping drastically past day 5, the pulp is soft. Cryo-freezers are run *before* the sugar conversion drops to negative values to lock in peak organoleptic compounds.
3. Alcohol Fermentation Prevention At day 7, the derivative turns negative ($f'(7) < 0$). This occurs as wild yeasts consume sucrose, fermenting it into ethanol and organic acids, reducing edible quality.
